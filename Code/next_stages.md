## Conceptual Note: Gender Norms, Income Shocks, and Female Labour Supply

### Core Question
This project studies how a **negative income shock** (CSSVD-related income loss post-2014) translates into **female labour supply responses**, and whether this translation is **mediated or constrained by gender norms operating within households and communities**.

The key idea is not simply whether women work more after the shock, but **which women respond, in what ways, and in which normative environments**.

---

### Conceptual Framework (Key Reminder)
In a frictionless household model, a **negative income shock should increase female labour supply** via income/necessity effects. If we observe:

- muted responses,
- delayed entry,
- sectorally constrained work,
- or heterogeneous responses by norm environment,

then this suggests the presence of **binding social norms acting as fixed constraints** on labour supply.

Importantly, this differs from the Field et al. (2021) setting:
- They identify norms via a *sign reversal* (income/control ↑ but FLFP ↑).
- Here, norms are identified via **suppressed or distorted adjustment** despite falling income.

---

### Two Distinct Norm Channels to Keep Straight

1. **Constraint / bargaining channel (norms bind)**
   - Norm costs remain high.
   - Income falls, but women cannot easily enter work because:
     - husbands control spending,
     - mobility is restricted,
     - provider norms bind.
   - Prediction: weak or selective labour response in norm-constrained households.

2. **Income-elastic stigma / norm relaxation channel**
   - Norm costs themselves decline when income falls.
   - Husbands may relax provider norms as survival constraints bind.
   - Interpretation: the *utility cost* of stigma is income-dependent.
   - Prediction: stronger female labour responses where income loss is severe, potentially even in norm-strict areas.

These are conceptually different mechanisms and should **not be conflated**.

---

### Empirical Strategy Reminder

- Cannot track individual women over time (DHS limitation).
- Treat gender norms as **local, slow-moving constraints** measured at the PSU/cluster level using **pre-shock data**.

Key objects:
- Baseline (pre-shock) PSU-level averages of:
  - male control over spending/purchases,
  - female decision-making power,
  - mobility restrictions,
  - attitudes to female work (if available).

Main estimand:
> Does the same negative income shock translate into different female labour outcomes depending on the **local norm environment**?

This is identified via interactions:
- income shock × baseline cluster-norm intensity.

---

### What to Look For When Reopening This Project

When coming back to the analysis, check:

1. **Heterogeneity**
   - Do women in norm-constrained clusters respond less or differently to the shock?
   - Is the extensive margin (entry) vs intensive margin (hours/sector) affected?

2. **Sectoral Patterns**
   - Are responses confined to “acceptable” sectors (family farm, unpaid, home-based)?
   - Is market-facing or non-agricultural work suppressed where norms are strong?

3. **Shock Severity**
   - Are effects stronger where income losses are larger?
   - Does this support income-elastic stigma vs fixed constraints?

4. **Timing**
   - Is there delayed entry, consistent with fixed norm costs being paid only after persistence?

5. **Robustness**
   - Control carefully for baseline poverty, education, rural isolation, and sectoral composition.
   - Ensure cluster norms are pre-shock and not mechanically affected by outcomes.

---

### One-Sentence Mental Anchor
> This project asks whether gender norms act as **conversion constraints** that weaken or distort households’ ability to use female labour as a coping mechanism following a negative income shock.

---

### Caution for Interpretation
- Do **not** claim individual norm change.
- Frame results as **environmental constraint heterogeneity**.
- Distinguish clearly between:
  - norms being *overridden* (necessity),
  - norms being *relaxed* (income-elastic stigma),
  - norms remaining binding (suppressed adjustment).

---

### Key Comparator in the Literature
Field et al. (2021, AER): norms inferred from unexpected labour entry after income/control gains.
This project: norms inferred from **failure or distortion of adjustment under negative income pressure**.
